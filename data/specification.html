1. Purpose

The purpose of CUDE is to provide a means for extracting, exchanging and importing data reliably and accurately from national and international geospatial databases worldwide. Many of these databases have mandates, either legislative or organizational, to make their data easily accessible for public use, free of charge.

As of yet, there is no single uniform method for accessing the data in bulk format for download. A myriad of issues still exist that have impeded this public endeavor. We've encountered the following:

  1. Data may only be available on a per record at a time using a text-based search.
  2. Data may only be available via an excel spreadsheet or PDF document.
  3. Data may be in old-style exchange formats, using inconsistent delimination:
    • Fixed Column Width
    • Tab deliminated
    • Pipe Symbol deliminated
  4. Data may not be indexed using non-standard codes vs. international standard, such as ISO 3166 codes for countries and administrative subdivisions.
  5. Relevant data from the same entity may be spread across plural databases, with inconsistent cross indexing.
  6. Records may be historical, out-of-date, or not descriptive enough to identify its feature designation accurately.

2. Methodology

[2.1] We propose using CSV (comma-separated-values) as a format for exchange. This has several advantages for wide access to application developers of various skills and platforms:

  • Easy to parse and describe the fields.
  • Data can be imported/exported to/from spreadsheets.
  • Conventional databases accept CSV format for import.
  • Flat file structure can be read and visually parsed by people.

[2.2] We further propose using the European standard for CSV where fields are deliminated by semi-colons (vs. commas). This assists in avoiding parsing conflicts with postal addressing and set fields which commonly use commas as separators. Use of semi-colons in data representation is fairly rare and provides a means for the least conflict between field separators and field values.

+ Show me an example.


value1; value2; value3

[2.3] We further propose that all integer values be represented in base ten as single numbers without thousandths unit separators. This avoids parsing conflicts between conventions used in the United States (comma) and other countries (e.g., semi-colon).

+ Show me an example.


1234; 40000; 99999

[2.4] We further propose that all decimal numbers must have at least one digit before the decimal point. Thus a number less than one would be represented with a leading '0.'. This assists in parsing in clearly identifying the decimal point from a punctuation number.

+ Show me an example.


0.001; 12.123; 16.45

[2.5] We further propose all signed numbers be preceded by a single plus ('+') or minus ('-') sign with no whitespace between the sign and the number.

+ Show me an example.


+42; -32.6; -0.005

[2.6] We further propose all numeric ranges be specified as a minimum and maximum value with a dash ('-') separating the minimum and maximum values.

+ Show me an example.


0-60 ; 100 - 2000 ; -0.01 - 0.01

[2.7] We further propose all values in a set of values for a field be separated by a pipe symbol ('|'). We choose not to use a comma due to the common occurence in values such as addresses and coordinates.

+ Show me an example.


one|two|three ; four|five|six

[2.8] We further propose all variant values for a field be separated by a forward slash ('/').

+ Show me an example.


Tom / Thomas / Tommy ; Sue / Susan / Suzie

[2.9] We further propose all coordinates be specified in decimal format (vs. degrees, minutes, seconds) which simplifies parsing. Each coordinate is specified as a latitude and longitude pair separated by a comma (',').

+ Show me an example.


10.1, -170 ; 46.123, -120.019

[2.9.1] We further propose a bounding box with a Northeast and Southwest corner is specified as a pair of coordinates (i.e., separated by '|').

+ Show me an example.


71.8 , 179.78 | 5.87 , -179.15

[2.10] We further propose specifying all measurement units in metrics, which avoids confusion on the measurement unit system.

+ Show me an example.


meters vs. feet
square kilometers (km2) vs. square miles

[2.11] We further propose all leading and trailing whitespace in a field value is ignored.

+ Show me an example.


12 ; 123 ;     is the same as   12;123;

[2.12] We further propose field qualifiers are added to the end of a field to designate some qualifier specific to the field. The supported global conventions will be:

  • [l:chars] Used to designate a language qualifier (e.g., [l:es] for Spanish).
  • [c:number] Used to designate a citation note (e.g. [c:2]).
  • [u:unit] Used to designage a unit multipler (e.g., [u:K] for Thousand). Added Nov. 1, 2013
  • [$:currency] Used to designate the currency of a monetary value. Uses the ISO-4127 standard for currency symbols. Added Nov. 6, 2013
  • [~] Used to designate an estimate (i.e., [~]).
Valid unit qualifiers are:
  • K Thousand
  • M Million
  • B Billion
  • T Trillion
  • KHa 1000 Hectare
  • SQM Square Meters
+ Show me an example.


Estados Unidos de América[l:es] # language qualifier
30000[~] # estimate qualiier
2009[c:13] # citation note qualifier
104[u:K] # unit qualifier

[2.13] We further propose qualifier annotations are added to the end of the qualifier term to designate some a numeric domain. The supported global conventions will be:

  • % Percent
  • * Per Person (Capita)
  • ** Per 1K Persons
  • *** Per 100K Persons
  • ^ Per Hectacre
  • + Male
  • - Female
+ Show me an example.


30000[rural+] # male domain to rural qualiier
6.2[death%] # percent domain to death qualifier
11[deathm**] # per 100K domain to maternal mortality qualifier

[2.14] We further propose a # is used to indicate a commnent line that extends to the end of the line.

+ Show me an example.


# this whole line is a comment
field1; field2; # the remainder of this line is a comment

[2.15] We additionally propose a fixed order of fields in the CSV file; whereby the fields can concisely and comprehensively describe features that are geographic, business, people, animals, etc.

+ Show me an example.


field1; field2; field3;....

[2.16] We further propose that the fields are segmented into ordered segments with a fixed order sequence per segment.

+ Show me an example.


segment1; segment2; segment3; ...
where segment = field1; field2; field3; ....

[2.17] We further propose that the sequence starts with a root segment; whereby, the root segment identifies the country and first level administrative division, using ISO 3166 codes, that the feature relates to, and feature classifications identifying the nature of the feature.

+ Show me an example.


root-segment; segment2; segment3; ...

[2.18] We further propose that the root segment is followed by a location segment whereby, the location segment provides geographic coordinate on where on a map to place the feature and area scope of the feature.

+ Show me an example.


root-segment; location-segment; segment3; ...

[2.19] We further propse that the location segment is followed by a name segment; whereby, the name segment provides name identification(s) of the feature specific to the local language of the location or root segments.

+ Show me an example.


root-segment; location-segment; name-segment3; segment4; ...

[2.20] We further propose that all name values be written in their proper case and character set.

+ Show me an example.


États-Unis d'Amérique # proper noun capitalization and character set for French

[2.21] We further propose that all name values be written as UTF-8 Unicode characters to support latin-based, cyrillic and asian eastern based character sets.

+ Show me an example.


Kaнaдa # Russian cyrillic characters

[2.22] We further propose that language identification is identified using ISO 639 language codes.

+ Show me an example.


en # English
es # Spanish
fr # French

[2.23] We further propose that the root, location and name segments are required in all records, and all remaining segments are optional.

+ Show me an example.


root-segment; location-segment; name-segment; # Required segments
root-segment; location-segment; name-segment; segment4; ... # Required followed by optional segments

[2.24] We further propose that requirements 2.1 to 2.23 must be meet for compliance to the base standard.

3. Specification

3.1 Layout

[3.1.1] The layout of segments in the CSV file is all follows, where bold are required and italic are optional segments:

Root   Location   Name   Address   Contact   Population   Resource   Scope   Extended

[3.1.2] Preceding segments must be specified for any optional segment specified. For example, if Contact segment is specified, then the preceding Address segment must be specified.

+ Show me an example.


Root   Location   Name   Address   Contact   # Valid
Root   Location   Name   Contact   # Invalid, Address segment does not proceed Contact

3.2 Root

[3.2.1] The root segment has the following field sequence:

CC ; SC ; ADM2; LOCODE; FC ; FD ; FX ; ID ;

CC = Country Code ISO 3166-1 alpha 2 (2-letter) code.
SC = Subdivision Code ISO 3166-2:2007 (2 or 3-letter) subdivision code.
ADM2  = 2nd Level Administrative Division Code No international standard code
LOCODE = Trade and Transport Location UN/LOCODE code
FC = Feature Class NGA Geonet Server GNS Feature Class (FC)
FD = Feature Designation Code NGA Geonet Server GNS Feature Designation (DSG)
FX  = Feature Description Extension Proprietary Extension
ID  = Feature Identifier Dataset(s) specific uniquer identifier for featuire

[3.2.2] All field values are required, except the A2, MC, FX and ID fields. Those fields may be left blank.

+ Show me an example.


US ; CA ; 037[fips]; LAX[iata] ; P ; PPL ; city ; 201334[gnis] ;

[3.2.3] CC is the International Standard Organization (ISO) 3166-1 alpha-2 standard for the assignment of 2-letter codes to countries, dependencies and territories recognized by the United Nations. citation

+ Show me an example.


US = United States
CA = Canada
MX = Mexico

[3.2.3.1] All CC values may be appended with one of or more of the following qualifiers enclosed within brackets.

+ Show me the qualifiers.
a3 = ISO 3166-1 alpha-3 country code
n3 = ISO 3166-1 numeric-3 country code
n3r = ISO 3166-1 numeric macro geographical (continental) region code
n3s = ISO 3166-1 numeric geographical sub-region code
fips = US FIPS 10-4 country code

+ Show me an example.
US|USA[a3]|840[n3]|US[fips]

[3.2.4] SC is the International Standard Organization (ISO) 3166-2:2007 standard for the assignment of 2 and 3 letter codes to first level administrative divisions (subdivisions) of the countries, dependencies and territories recognized by the United Nations. citation

+ Show me an example.


NY = New York, US
BC = British Columbia, CA
ACT = Australian Capital Territory, AU

[3.2.4.1] All SC values may be appended with one of or more of the following qualifiers enclosed within brackets.

+ Show me the qualifiers.
fips = US FIPS 5-2 state code
conv = Country specific conventional abbreviation

+ Show me an example.
NY|36[fips]

[3.2.5] A2 is for codes identiying 2nd level administrative divisions. There is no standard code for the base.

[3.2.5.1] All A2 values may be appended with one of or more of the following qualifiers enclosed within brackets.

+ Show me the qualifiers.
fips = US FIPS 6-4 (INCITS 31:200x) county code

+ Show me an example.
047[fips]

[3.2.6] LOCODE is the United Nations Code for Trade and Transport Locations for locations which function as seaports, rail and road terminals, airports, post offices and border crossings citation.

+ Show me the qualifiers.

fips = US FIPS 55-3 code for named populated place
iata = International Air Transport Association code
icao = International Civil Aviation Organization code
+ Show me an example.
LAX|SNA[iata] # Los Angeles (LOCODE) | John Wayne Airport (IATA)

3.3 Location

[3.3.1] The location segment specifies the geographic location of the entity and most follow the root segment. The location segment has the following field sequence:

COORD ; ELV ; LVL ; AREA ; WTR ; INH ;

COORD = Coordinate WGS84 Latitude, Longitude.
ELV = Elevation Elevation in meters.
LVL = Level Level in multiple level entity (e.g., building).
AREA = Land Area Area of physical terrain covered by entity.
WTR = Water Area Area of physical terrain covered by entity which is covered by water.
INH = Inhabited Area Amount of area inhabited, includes inhabited levels (e.g., floors).

[3.3.2] All field values are optional and may be left blank.

+ Show me an example.


45.5, -122.7 ; 200 ; 0 ; 12 ; 1 ; 10 ;

[3.3.3] COORD is the World Geodetic Standard (WGS) 84, last revised in 2004, for specifying coordinates and altitude data for Earth. Coordinates are specified as a latitude and longitude pair separated by a comma. The coordinates may be specified either in decimal (DEC) or degrees, minutes, seconds (DMS) format. The coordinate is the area centroid of the feature.

+ Show me an example.

45.5, -122.7 # decimal (DEC) format
45° 30' 0", -122° 0' 0" # degrees, minutes, seconds (DMS) format
45° 30' 0" N, 122° 0' 0" W # degrees, minutes, seconds (DMS) format

[3.3.3.1] All COORD values may be appended with one or more qualifiers enclosed within brackets.

+ Show me the qualifiers.

pop = population centroid
src = source (origin)
nac = Natural Area Coding (NAC) coordinate system
+ Show me an example.
45.5, -122.7[roof]

[3.3.4] ELV is the elevation above sea level and is specified in meters. Elevation may be specified either as a single value for the tallest point or as a dash separated pair for the tallest and lowest points respectively. When specifying the lowest point without a tallest point, the tallest point is left blank.

+ Show me an example.

-10 # 10 meters below sea level(tallest)
36 - -10 # 36 meters above seal level (tallest) and 10 meters below sea level (lowest)
- -10 # 10 meters below sea level(lowest)

[3.3.5] LVL is the level when specifying within a multi-level entity. This is primarily used for floors in buildings. Level may be specified either as a single level or a range of levels.

1 Ground or First Floor
2 2nd Floor
999 Rooftop
